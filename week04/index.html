Worksheet 4 Solutions

Part 1: Photon Emission

Given: Power $P = 25\text{ W}$, Efficiency $\eta = 20\% = 0.2$, Wavelength $\lambda = 500\text{ nm}$.

1. Calculate Radiant Flux ($\Phi_e$):

$$ \Phi_e = P \cdot \eta = 25\text{ W} \cdot 0.2 = 5\text{ W} $$

2. Calculate energy of a single photon ($E_p$):

$$ E_p = \frac{h \cdot c}{\lambda} $$ $$ E_p = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34}\text{ J}\cdot\text{s}) \cdot (3.0 \times 10^8\text{ m/s})}{500 \times 10^{-9}\text{ m}} $$ $$ E_p \approx 3.976 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J} $$

3. Calculate number of photons per second ($N$):

$$ N = \frac{\Phi_e}{E_p} = \frac{5}{3.976 \times 10^{-19}} $$

Answer: $N \approx 1.26 \times 10^{19} \text{ photons/s}$

Part 2: Light Bulb Radiometry

Given: $V = 2.4\text{ V}$, $I = 0.7\text{ A}$, Diameter $d = 1\text{ cm} = 0.01\text{ m}$.

1. Radiant Flux ($\Phi_e$):

$$ \Phi_e = V \cdot I = 2.4 \cdot 0.7 = 1.68\text{ W} $$

2. Radiant Intensity ($I_e$):

Assuming isotropic emission ($\Omega = 4\pi\text{ sr}$): $$ I_e = \frac{\Phi_e}{4\pi} = \frac{1.68}{4\pi} \approx 0.134\text{ W/sr} $$

3. Radiant Exitance ($M_e$):

Surface area of sphere $A = \pi d^2 = \pi (0.01)^2 \approx 3.14 \times 10^{-4}\text{ m}^2$. $$ M_e = \frac{\Phi_e}{A} = \frac{1.68}{\pi \cdot 10^{-4}} \approx 5347.6\text{ W/m}^2 $$

4. Emitted Energy in 5 minutes ($Q$):

$$ Q = \Phi_e \cdot t = 1.68\text{ W} \cdot (5 \cdot 60\text{ s}) = 1.68 \cdot 300 $$

Answer: $Q = 504\text{ J}$

Part 3: Irradiance at the Eye

Given: Distance $D = 1\text{ m}$.

Using the inverse square law for a point source approximation:

$$ E = \frac{I_e}{D^2} $$ $$ E = \frac{0.134\text{ W/sr}}{(1\text{ m})^2} $$

Answer: $E \approx 0.134\text{ W/m}^2$

Part 4: Irradiance & Illuminance

Given: Power $200\text{ W}$, Efficiency $0.2$, Distance $2\text{ m}$, $\lambda = 650\text{ nm}$.

1. Radiant Flux:

$$ \Phi_e = 200 \cdot 0.2 = 40\text{ W} $$

2. Radiant Intensity:

$$ I_e = \frac{40}{4\pi} \approx 3.183\text{ W/sr} $$

3. Irradiance at table ($E_e$):

$$ E_e = \frac{I_e}{D^2} = \frac{3.183}{2^2} \approx 0.796\text{ W/m}^2 $$

4. Illuminance ($E_v$):

Formula: $E_v = E_e \cdot 685 \cdot V(\lambda)$. At $650\text{ nm}$, $V(\lambda) = 0.1$. $$ E_v = 0.796 \cdot 685 \cdot 0.1 $$

Answer: $E_v \approx 54.5\text{ lux}$

Part 5: Photometry (Bunsen Photometer)

Given: $I_s = 40\text{ cd}$, $d_s = 0.35\text{ m}$, $d_x = 1.0 - 0.35 = 0.65\text{ m}$.

At the position of match, illuminance is equal ($E_s = E_x$):

$$ \frac{I_s}{d_s^2} = \frac{I_x}{d_x^2} $$ $$ I_x = I_s \left( \frac{d_x}{d_s} \right)^2 $$ $$ I_x = 40 \left( \frac{0.65}{0.35} \right)^2 = 40 \cdot (1.857)^2 $$

Answer: $I_x \approx 138\text{ cd}$

Part 6: Diffuse Emitter

Given: Area $10\times10\text{ cm} = 0.01\text{ m}^2$, Radiance $L = 5000\text{ W/(sr m}^2\text{)}$.

1. Radiant Exitance ($M_e$):

For a Lambertian (diffuse) surface, $M = \pi L$. $$ M_e = \pi \cdot 5000 \approx 15708\text{ W/m}^2 $$

2. Total Power ($\Phi_e$):

$$ \Phi_e = M_e \cdot A = 15708 \cdot 0.01 $$

Answer: $\Phi_e \approx 157\text{ W}$

Part 7: Non-Diffuse Emitter

Given: $L(\theta) = 6000 \cos(\theta)$, Area $A = 0.01\text{ m}^2$.

1. Radiant Exitance ($M_e$):

Integrate over the hemisphere: $$ M_e = \int_{0}^{2\pi} \int_{0}^{\pi/2} L(\theta) \cos(\theta) \sin(\theta) \, d\theta \, d\phi $$ $$ M_e = \int_{0}^{2\pi} d\phi \int_{0}^{\pi/2} 6000 \cos^2(\theta) \sin(\theta) \, d\theta $$ $$ M_e = 2\pi \cdot 6000 \left[ -\frac{\cos^3(\theta)}{3} \right]_{0}^{\pi/2} $$ $$ M_e = 12000\pi \cdot \frac{1}{3} = 4000\pi \approx 12566\text{ W/m}^2 $$

2. Total Power ($\Phi_e$):

$$ \Phi_e = M_e \cdot A = 12566 \cdot 0.01 $$

Answer: $\Phi_e \approx 125.7\text{ W}$

Part 8: Sun, Earth & Mars

Given: Sun Power $\Phi = 3.91 \cdot 10^{26}\text{ W}$, Area $A_{\text{sun}} = 6.07 \cdot 10^{18}\text{ m}^2$.
$D_{\text{earth}} = 1.5 \cdot 10^{11}\text{ m}$, $D_{\text{mars}} = 2.28 \cdot 10^{11}\text{ m}$.

1. Radiance Comparison:

Radiance ($L$) is conserved along a ray in a vacuum. Therefore, the Radiance of the Sun is identical when observed from both planets.

Earth is warmest because it is closer, resulting in a larger solid angle $\Omega$ and thus higher Irradiance ($E = L \cdot \Omega$).

2. Calculate Radiance ($L$):

Since the Sun is a diffuse emitter, $M = \pi L$. First, find Exitance $M$:

$$ M = \frac{\Phi}{A_{\text{sun}}} = \frac{3.91 \cdot 10^{26}}{6.07 \cdot 10^{18}} \approx 6.44 \cdot 10^{7} \text{ W/m}^2 $$ $$ L = \frac{M}{\pi} \approx \frac{6.44 \cdot 10^{7}}{\pi} \approx 2.05 \cdot 10^{7} \text{ W/(sr m}^2) $$

Radiance $L \approx 2.05 \cdot 10^{7} \text{ W/(sr m}^2)$ (Same for Earth & Mars)

3. Solid Angle ($\Omega$):

Projected Area of Sun $A_{\text{proj}} = \frac{A_{\text{sun}}}{4} = 1.5175 \cdot 10^{18} \text{ m}^2$.

$$ \Omega_{\text{earth}} = \frac{A_{\text{proj}}}{D_{\text{earth}}^2} = \frac{1.5175 \cdot 10^{18}}{(1.5 \cdot 10^{11})^2} \approx 6.74 \cdot 10^{-5} \text{ sr} $$ $$ \Omega_{\text{mars}} = \frac{A_{\text{proj}}}{D_{\text{mars}}^2} = \frac{1.5175 \cdot 10^{18}}{(2.28 \cdot 10^{11})^2} \approx 2.92 \cdot 10^{-5} \text{ sr} $$

4. Energy Received (Irradiance $E$):

Assuming Sun at zenith ($\cos \theta = 1$): $E = L \cdot \Omega$.

$$ E_{\text{earth}} = 2.05 \cdot 10^{7} \cdot 6.74 \cdot 10^{-5} \approx 1382 \text{ W/m}^2 $$ $$ E_{\text{mars}} = 2.05 \cdot 10^{7} \cdot 2.92 \cdot 10^{-5} \approx 599 \text{ W/m}^2 $$

Earth: ~1382 W, Mars: ~599 W (on 1x1m surface)

Part 9: Cloudy Day & Conical Section

Given: Uniform Radiance $L = 1000 \text{ W/(m}^2 \text{ sr)}$.

1. Full Sky Irradiance:

For uniform radiance over the hemisphere, $E = \pi L$.

$$ E = \pi \cdot 1000 \approx 3141.6 \text{ W/m}^2 $$

2. Blocked Sky (Conical Section, half-angle $30^\circ$):

We integrate radiance over the cone from $\theta = 0$ to $30^\circ$.

$$ E = \int_{0}^{2\pi} d\phi \int_{0}^{30^\circ} L \cos \theta \sin \theta \, d\theta $$ $$ E = 2\pi L \left[ \frac{\sin^2 \theta}{2} \right]_{0}^{30^\circ} = \pi L \sin^2(30^\circ) $$

Since $\sin(30^\circ) = 0.5$:

$$ E = \pi \cdot 1000 \cdot (0.5)^2 = 1000\pi \cdot 0.25 $$ $$ E = 250\pi \approx 785.4 \text{ W/m}^2 $$

Answer: $E \approx 785.4 \text{ W/m}^2$
